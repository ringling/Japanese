Today's session

Friday is quiz day. The full ~30 minutes of kanji effort goes into active retrieval — testing what you actually remember rather than rereading what you've already seen. Active retrieval is the strongest single learning action: it's worth more than five passive re-readings of the same material.

The structure is three passes through all 13 of this week's kanji:

Pass 1 — Meanings: see the kanji, recall what it means.
Pass 2 — Readings: see the kanji, recall both on'yomi and kun'yomi.
Pass 3 — Production: see English, recall the kanji.

If a kanji feels shaky, that's information — not failure. Note it. Saturday's review will surface it again.

After the quiz, a small supplemental vocabulary set on age and counting people rounds out the day in ~10 minutes.

PASS 02

Readings — see kanji, recall both readings

~10 min

This pass is harder. For each kanji, you need to recall the on'yomi (音, in katakana convention) and the kun'yomi (訓, in hiragana). Some kanji have multiple of each — get the most common one and you've passed.

For each kanji: state both readings aloud.

If you blank on the kun'yomi but get the on'yomi, that's a half-pass — fine for now.
Go group-by-group rather than all at once. Five at a time, check the answers, then move on. Forcing recall before seeing the answer is what builds memory.

PASS 03

Production — see English, write the kanji

~8 min

The hardest pass. Recognition (passive) is far easier than production (active). The JLPT only tests recognition, but practicing production sharpens your recognition by forcing you to notice the visual differences between similar kanji (十 vs 千, for example).

Take a piece of paper. For each English word, write the kanji.

Pen and paper required. No typing.
Don't just visualize — actually write. The motor act is what locks the kanji shape into long-term memory. Write each kanji once, even the easy ones (一, 二, 三 are fine but write them anyway).

PART 04

Vocabulary supplement — Age & Counting people

~10 min

A small additional set today, building on Wednesday's numbers/money vocabulary. These words use the people-counting irregularities you've been drilling all week (ひとり, ふたり, etc.), so it's coherent reinforcement rather than new ground.

Counting people

The first two are irregular — ひとり and ふたり use native readings, not the on'yomi. From three onwards, the pattern becomes regular (〜にん).

WordReadingEnglish
一人ひとりone person / alone (irregular)
二人ふたりtwo people (irregular)
三人さんにんthree people
四人よにんfour people (note: よ, not よん)
何人なんにんhow many people?
Marugoto link: you've used these in restaurant scenes — 3人です ("three people") on entry to a restaurant. The form is familiar; today is just locking it in for testing.

Age expressions

Asking and stating age uses the counter 〜歳 (さい). Most readings are regular except 20 — that's a special cultural milestone with its own irregular reading.

WordReadingEnglish
〜歳〜さい~ years old (counter)
何歳なんさいhow old? (polite enough for most contexts)
おいくつおいくつhow old? (more polite)
二十歳はたち20 years old (special reading!)
大人おとなadult
子供こどもchild
Cultural note: 二十歳 (はたち) is the traditional age of adulthood in Japan. While the legal age has shifted to 18 for some purposes, the kanji 二十歳 with its special reading is still used for Coming-of-Age Day (成人の日) — a national holiday for those turning 20. The irregular reading marks the cultural significance.
