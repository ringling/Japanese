Today's session

You're meeting seven kanji for the first time: 一 二 三 四 五 六 七. The numbers one through seven. Several of these you almost certainly already know — that's intentional. Week 1 is meant to feel easy. The point is to build the habit and rhythm of sitting down to study, not to overload day one.

For each kanji you'll see: the character itself, its meanings, the on'yomi (音, in katakana, the Chinese-origin reading) and kun'yomi (訓, in hiragana, the native Japanese reading), a memory hook, stroke count, and example words.

Today's job is recognition, not reproduction. Don't try to write them or memorize all the readings. Just look. Read each card slowly. Say the meaning aloud. Say the readings aloud. Move on. Wednesday you'll write them; Friday you'll quiz yourself.

KANJI 01

一 — one

~3 min
一
Number 1 of 104
one
音イチ・イツ
訓ひと(つ)

Mnemonic

A single horizontal stroke. The simplest possible mark in Japanese. One stroke = one.

Stroke count

1 stroke (drawn left to right)

Example words

  • 一いち— one (number, abstract)
  • 一ひとつ— one (item, native counter)
  • 一ひと人り— one person / alone
  • 一いち月がつ— January
Note: 一 is read いち in most counting and date contexts (一月 = January, 一時 = 1 o'clock), but ひとつ when counting general items (りんご一つ = one apple). In 一人 it irregularly reads ひとり.
KANJI 02

二 — two

~3 min
二
Number 2 of 104
two
音ニ
訓ふた(つ)

Mnemonic

Two horizontal strokes. The lower stroke is slightly longer. Two strokes = two.

Stroke count

2 strokes (top first, then bottom)

Example words

  • 二に— two (number)
  • 二ふたつ— two (items)
  • 二ふた人り— two people
  • 二に月がつ— February
Watch out: 二 is easy to confuse with the equals sign (=) when written quickly. Make sure the lower stroke is clearly longer than the upper.
KANJI 03

三 — three

~3 min
三
Number 3 of 104
three
音サン
訓みっ(つ) / み(つ)

Mnemonic

Three horizontal strokes — middle stroke shortest, bottom longest. Three strokes = three. The pattern of strokes-to-meaning continues from 一 and 二.

Stroke count

3 strokes (top to bottom)

Example words

  • 三さん— three (number)
  • 三みっつ— three (items)
  • 三さん人にん— three people
  • 三さん月がつ— March
Pattern break: The strokes-equals-number trick stops working at 4. So 三 is the last "intuitive" number kanji — past this, you need to learn shapes.
KANJI 04

四 — four

~3 min
四
Number 4 of 104
four
音シ
訓よん / よっ(つ) / よ

Mnemonic

A box with two legs sticking down inside it — like a small enclosure with four corners. Or: a window with a person standing inside it.

Stroke count

5 strokes

Example words

  • 四よん— four (most common reading)
  • 四よっつ— four (items)
  • 四よ時じ— 4 o'clock (note: not しじ!)
  • 四し月がつ— April
Cultural note: The on'yomi シ sounds identical to 死 (し, "death"), so Japanese speakers prefer よん in most contexts. The number 4 is considered unlucky — many hospitals skip the 4th floor, and gift sets avoid groupings of 4.
KANJI 05

五 — five

~3 min
五
Number 5 of 104
five
音ゴ
訓いつ(つ)

Mnemonic

An hourglass-like shape. Or: a hand frozen mid-count showing five fingers. The kanji originally depicted two crossing lines representing the five elements meeting at a center.

Stroke count

4 strokes

Example words

  • 五ご— five (number)
  • 五いつつ— five (items)
  • 五ご時じ— 5 o'clock
  • 五ご月がつ— May
Memory aid: 五 only has one common on'yomi (ゴ) and one kun'yomi (いつつ) — it's one of the more straightforward numbers.
KANJI 06

六 — six

~3 min
六
Number 6 of 104
six
音ロク
訓むっ(つ) / む(つ)

Mnemonic

A pointy hat sitting on top of two legs. A house with a roof and supports — six elements making up a complete structure.

Stroke count

4 strokes

Example words

  • 六ろく— six (number)
  • 六むっつ— six (items)
  • 六ろく時じ— 6 o'clock
  • 六ろく月がつ— June
Sound change preview: When 六 combines with certain counters, the く drops and doubles the next sound: 六本 = ろっぽん (six long objects). You'll drill these explicitly later in the week.
KANJI 07

七 — seven

~3 min
七
Number 7 of 104
seven
音シチ
訓なな / なな(つ)

Mnemonic

Imagine the number 7 mirrored, with a hook at the bottom. Or: a fishing hook curving back on itself.

Stroke count

2 strokes

Example words

  • 七なな / 七しち— seven (both common)
  • 七ななつ— seven (items)
  • 七しち時じ— 7 o'clock
  • 七しち月がつ— July
Reading split: なな is more common in everyday speech (especially when reading numbers aloud or specifying quantities), while しち is used for time (七時) and months (七月). Like 四, the on'yomi シチ overlaps with another sound (一 = イチ) so people sometimes say なな to avoid confusion.
