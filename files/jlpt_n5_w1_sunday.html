Step away

Today you do nothing. That is the instruction.

The 48–72 hour gap between Saturday's review and Monday's new material is not wasted time — it is when your brain moves what you practised from short-term working memory into long-term storage. Forcing more input now actively competes with that process.

Rest is not a reward for finishing the week. It is part of the method. The schedule was designed with Sunday off. Trust it.

If you feel restless: go outside, read something in any language, notice if any of this week's kanji appear on packaging, a menu, or an app. That passive contact is fine. Drilling is not.

OPTIONAL

Kanji in the wild

0 min required

If you feel like it — a 30-second exercise

Next time you encounter any Japanese text today — a food label, a restaurant menu, a phone app, a film subtitle — glance at it and see if you can spot any of the 13 kanji from this week.

一 二 三 四 五 六 七 八 九 十 百 千 万 — they appear constantly: prices, quantities, dates, phone numbers, times.

You don't need to write anything down or make a lesson of it. Just notice. That passive recognition is the beginning of reading Japanese naturally.

Coming Monday — Week 2

Time & Calendar · 時・暦

日 月 火 水 木 金 土

Seven kanji that form the days of the week. Each represents a natural element: sun, moon, fire, water, wood, gold, earth — assigned to the days by ancient Chinese cosmology. Once you know these seven elements, you can read any day of the week in Japanese.

The pictographic logic is strong here. 木 looks like a tree. 水 looks like flowing water. 火 looks like flames. These will mostly stick on first exposure — Week 2 Monday should feel lighter than Week 1 Monday.
