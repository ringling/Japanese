Shape + meaning + story

Today's session

You're meeting seven kanji that form the days of the week: 日 月 火 水 木 金 土. These are the seven natural elements — sun, moon, fire, water, wood, gold, earth — each assigned to a day of the week by ancient Chinese cosmology. Once you know these seven, you can read any day of the week in Japanese.

You already know 月 from Week 1 (January = 一月, etc.). Here 月 doubles as the word for "moon" and gives Monday its name: 月曜日 = moon day = Monday. The same logic runs through all seven.

Today's job is recognition, not reproduction. Look at each character, learn the element it represents, and say the day name aloud. Wednesday you'll reinforce these and add the six remaining time kanji (曜 年 時 分 半 今).

KANJI 14

日 — sun / day

~3 min
日
Number 14 of 104
sun · day
音ニチ・ジツ
訓ひ・か

Mnemonic

The original pictograph of the sun — a circle with a horizontal line through the middle, representing the sun's core or a sun spot. A window looking directly at the sun.

Stroke count

4 strokes

Example words

  • 日曜日にちようび— Sunday (sun-day)
  • 今日きょう— today (irregular reading)
  • 毎日まいにち— every day
  • 何日なんにち— what day of the month
  • 日本にほん— Japan (origin-of-the-sun)
Key irregular: 今日 reads きょう in everyday speech, not こんにち. You'll see こんにち only frozen in こんにちは (good afternoon). Don't say こんにちにち.
KANJI 15

月 — moon / month

~3 min
月
Number 15 of 104
moon · month
音ゲツ・ガツ
訓つき

Mnemonic

A crescent moon. The original pictograph showed a crescent with a single dot inside. Now simplified to two short horizontal strokes inside a rounded frame — the moon's craters, perhaps.

Stroke count

4 strokes

Example words

  • 月曜日げつようび— Monday (moon-day)
  • 今月こんげつ— this month
  • 来月らいげつ— next month
  • 月つき— the moon
  • 一月いちがつ— January
Three readings in play: ゲツ for Monday and time expressions (来月, 今月); ガツ after numbers for month names (一月, 二月…); つき when meaning the physical moon. You already know all the month-name uses from Week 1 — now add the Monday and moon meanings.
KANJI 16

火 — fire

~3 min
火
Number 16 of 104
fire
音カ
訓ひ

Mnemonic

Flames leaping upward from a fire. The bottom two strokes are sparks flying outward. A campfire viewed head-on. The original pictograph was even more obviously a flame shape — look at it sideways.

Stroke count

4 strokes

Example words

  • 火曜日かようび— Tuesday (fire-day)
  • 火ひ— fire (as a thing)
  • 花火はなび— fireworks (flower + fire)
  • 火山かざん— volcano
Sound change: 花火 (fireworks) reads はなび — the ひ softens to び when it follows は. This voiced-consonant shift (ひ→び) happens frequently in compound words. Fireworks are literally "flower-fires."
KANJI 17

水 — water

~3 min
水
Number 17 of 104
water
音スイ
訓みず

Mnemonic

A central vertical stroke with three droplets and a flowing stream. A river seen from above with ripples spreading outward. The original pictograph showed a winding waterway with tributary streams branching off each side.

Stroke count

4 strokes

Example words

  • 水曜日すいようび— Wednesday (water-day)
  • 水みず— water
  • お水おみず— water (polite, e.g. in a restaurant)
  • 水泳すいえい— swimming
Radical note: When 水 appears as a left-side component (氵, three dots), it signals "relating to water" — like 海 (sea), 湖 (lake), 泳 (swim). The three dots are the compressed form of 水. Watch for it everywhere.
KANJI 18

木 — tree / wood

~3 min
木
Number 18 of 104
tree · wood
音モク・ボク
訓き・こ

Mnemonic

A tree in cross-section: vertical trunk (center stroke), spreading branches (upper horizontal stroke), and roots (two strokes at the bottom). A cross with roots — a tree. One of the most pictographic kanji still in common use.

Stroke count

4 strokes

Example words

  • 木曜日もくようび— Thursday (wood-day)
  • 木き— tree
  • 木村きむら— Kimura (very common surname: tree + village)
  • 木造もくぞう— wooden (structure)
Radical watch: Like 水→氵, when 木 appears on the left side of a kanji (木へん), it signals "made of wood" or "relating to trees" — 林 (grove), 森 (forest), 机 (desk), 椅子 (chair). You'll recognize it instantly after this week.
KANJI 19

金 — gold / money

~3 min
金
Number 19 of 104
gold · money
音キン・コン
訓かね・かな

Mnemonic

This week's most complex shape. Originally: a person (人) buried under a mound (土) with gold nuggets around them — like a buried treasure. Today it looks like a compound shape with a protective roof over it. Valuable things take more strokes to write.

Stroke count

8 strokes (most strokes of the week)

Example words

  • 金曜日きんようび— Friday (gold-day)
  • お金かね— money (everyday word)
  • 金きん— gold (the metal)
  • 金色きんいろ— golden (color)
  • 金属きんぞく— metal (any metal)
Double duty: 金 is the only kanji this week with two very distinct meanings in daily life — gold (the precious metal) and money in general. お金 (おかね) is how you say "money" in any normal conversation.
KANJI 20

土 — earth / soil

~3 min
土
Number 20 of 104
earth · soil
音ド・ト
訓つち

Mnemonic

A cross (+) with a longer horizontal base — like a seedling pushing up through the ground. The bottom stroke is the earth; the cross is the plant sprouting upward. With only 3 strokes, it's the simplest character since 三.

Stroke count

3 strokes

Example words

  • 土曜日どようび— Saturday (earth-day)
  • 土つち— soil / earth (physical)
  • 土地とち— land / plot
  • 土曜どよう— Saturday (abbreviated, no 日)
ド vs ト: ど in 土曜日 (Saturday), と in 土地 (とち, land). Both are on'yomi, just different compounds. つち when talking about actual soil — "the dirt under your feet."
REFERENCE

Days of the Week

Learn this table

All seven days follow the same pattern: [element] + 曜日 (ようび). The kanji 曜 (ようび) means "day of the week" — you'll study it on Wednesday. For now, just recognise the seven elements and their day names.

Element Day name Reading English
月 月曜日 げつようび Monday
火 火曜日 かようび Tuesday
水 水曜日 すいようび Wednesday
木 木曜日 もくようび Thursday
金 金曜日 きんようび Friday
土 土曜日 どようび Saturday
日 日曜日 にちようび Sunday

きょうは なんようびですか (What day is today?) · さんがつ はつか (March 20th — 20日 = はつか, irregular!) · らいしゅうの げつようび (next Monday) · まいしゅう どようび (every Saturday) · せんしゅうの きんようび (last Friday)

Done. Seven elements, seven days.

These seven kanji unlock every date and schedule you'll encounter in Japanese. They appear in calendars, train timetables, event posters, TV guides — everywhere. By the end of this week you'll read them without thinking.

If only one thing sticks from today, let it be the order: 月 火 水 木 金 土 日 — Moon, Fire, Water, Wood, Gold, Earth, Sun. Say it aloud a few times. It's the Japanese week.

Wednesday's plan

Reinforce these 7, introduce 曜 年 時 分 半 今, write each by hand, and start drilling time expressions — 何時ですか (what time is it?), ～から～まで (from X to Y), 今何月何日 (what's today's date).
