Today's session

Tuesday is grammar day. Today's focus is counters and number readings — both flagged as actual gaps in your diagnostic, and both essentially guaranteed test items on the JLPT N5.

Counters are the small suffixes Japanese attaches to numbers depending on what you're counting (one apple vs one cat vs one piece of paper vs one floor of a building all use different counters). The catch is that the readings change unpredictably — 一本 isn't いちほん, it's いっぽん. Same with the hundreds: 三百 isn't さんひゃく, it's さんびゃく.

This is pure memorization territory — there's no reasoning your way through it. Today you build the table, drill it, and lock it in.

PART 01

The general counter ～つ

~5 min

The native Japanese counter for general items 1–10. Use this when you don't know the proper counter, or when counting "items" in a generic way (apples, ideas, suggestions, courses on a menu). It's the safest fallback.

[Native number] + つ
general counter for 1 to 10

Pattern
Notice how 3, 4, 6, 8 all have a small っ before つ. This is doubling — the natural compression of two consonants meeting. 10 is the odd one — it drops the つ entirely and reads とお.

You've seen this in your Marugoto sessions already — ひとつずつ おねがいします ("one of each, please") used the ～つ counter. The form is familiar; today you're just locking it in for testing.

PART 03

Counter for long objects: ～本 (ほん)

~5 min

Used for cylindrical/long things: pens, bottles, umbrellas, trees, train tracks, phone calls (yes, those count), banana, fingers. This is where sound changes get aggressive.

いっぽん · さんぼん · ろっぽん · はっぽん · じゅっぽん
the rule: ほん becomes either ぽん (after っ) or ぼん (after ん)
Long-object counter ～本

The rule
The same pattern (1, 6, 8, 10 → っぽ · 3 → んぼ) applies to many other counters that start with the h-row sounds (は・ひ・ふ・へ・ほ). It's the single most important sound-change rule to memorize. Once you know it for ～本, you basically know it for ～匹, ～杯, ～分, ～ぴき, etc.
PART 04

Quick reference: other key counters

~6 min

You don't need to memorize the full table for every counter today — just get familiar with the most common ones and recognize when sound changes happen.

Counter ～枚 (まい) — flat objects (paper, tickets, plates, shirts)

1一枚いちまいregular
2二枚にまいregular
3三枚さんまいregular
10十枚じゅうまいregular — no sound change!

～枚 has no sound changes. If a counter starts with m, n, r, w, or y, you're usually safe — sound changes mostly hit the h-row and a few others.

Counter ～匹 (ひき) — small animals (cats, dogs, fish, insects)
#KanjiReadingNotes
1一匹いっぴき→ っぴ
2二匹にひきregular
3三匹さんびき→ んび
6六匹ろっぴき→ っぴ
10十匹じゅっぴき→ っぴ

Same rule as ～本 — 1, 6, 8, 10 doubles to っぴ; 3 voices to んび. Hぃ → びorぴ.

Counter ～回 (かい) — number of times
#KanjiReadingNotes
1一回いっかい→ doubles っか
2二回にかいregular
3三回さんかいregular (no voicing!)
6六回ろっかい→ doubles っか
10十回じゅっかい→ doubles っか
Useful overlap
～階 (kai, "floor") follows the exact same pattern as ～回 (kai, "times"). If you can say "second floor", you can say "two times" — same sounds. The kanji differ but the pronunciation rules are identical.
PART 05

The irregular hundreds (百)

~5 min

This is the second confirmed gap from your diagnostic. The hundreds (百, ひゃく) follow predictable patterns except at 300, 600, and 800 — where the sounds shift in ways you simply have to memorize.

Three to memorize, that's it
Only 300, 600, 800 are irregular. Memorize: さんびゃく · ろっぴゃく · はっぴゃく. Everything else is just the digit + ひゃく.

The thousands (千 = せん) have similar irregularities at 3000 and 8000 — but those are smaller test items for N5. Focus today on getting the hundreds locked in.

Don't say just まん
10,000 is always いちまん, never just まん. Unlike 100 (ひゃく) and 1,000 (せん) which can stand alone, 万 always needs a number in front of it.
DRILL

Active practice

~6 min

These drills are designed to be done out loud. Reading silently doesn't drill the irregular sound changes — your mouth needs to practice ろっぴゃく to lock it in.

Drill 1 · Read these prices aloud

Imagine you're at a Japanese restaurant or shop. Read each price aloud in Japanese.

  1. 三百円
  2. 六百円
  3. 八百円
  4. 千円
  5. 三千円
  6. 一万円
  7. 二千五百円
さんびゃくえん · ろっぴゃくえん · はっぴゃくえん · せんえん · さんぜんえん · いちまんえん · にせんごひゃくえん

Drill 2 · Counter selection

Which counter would you use? Match the item with the right counter.

  1. Three pencils (long object) → 三＿
  2. Two cats (small animal) → 二＿
  3. Five sheets of paper (flat) → 五＿
  4. Six people → 六＿
  5. One time / once → 一＿
  6. Three apples (general items) → 三＿
さんぼん (本) · にひき (匹) · ごまい (枚) · ろくにん (人) · いっかい (回) · みっつ (つ)

Drill 4 · Build a sentence

Use the patterns you know. Build each sentence aloud, no peeking.

  • "There are three people." (use いる + 三人)
  • "I bought two pens." (use 買いました + 二本 + ペンを)
  • "The cake is 600 yen." (use ケーキは + です + 六百円)
  • "Please give me one (of those)." (use 一つ + ください)
三人います · ペンを二本買いました · ケーキは六百円です · 一つください

Two real gaps, closed.

You started this session with two confirmed weak points: counter sound changes and irregular hundred-readings. By drilling them aloud today, you've moved them from "things I'd lose points on" to "things I can recognize and produce."

These are not patterns you "understand once and remember forever" — they fade if you don't revisit them. Slip a quick mental quiz into your week: when you see prices on Danish receipts or product tags, mentally read them in Japanese. 35 kr = さんじゅうごクローネ. It's free practice.
