Today's session

Wednesday is the heaviest single day in the weekly cycle — you do three things: reinforce Monday's seven kanji through handwriting, meet the remaining six numbers (八 九 十 百 千 万), and add the first vocabulary set on numbers and money.

The rhythm: Monday's kanji had 48 hours to settle in your memory. Today you write them by hand to lock in the visual + motor pathway, then add the new ones while that fresh memory consolidates Monday's. Writing matters here — recognition and production use slightly different pathways, and stroke order awareness only develops through actually moving your pen.

The vocabulary section is small (~12 words today) and tightly themed to the kanji — numbers, prices, and money expressions. Many you'll already know from Marugoto restaurant scenes.

PART 01

Reinforce — write Monday's seven by hand

~10 min

You'll need paper and a pen. Don't skip this — it's the most important kanji-strengthening activity of the week. Recognition (reading) and production (writing) live in different parts of the brain. The JLPT only tests recognition, but writing trains your brain to see the kanji more accurately, which makes recognition faster and more reliable.

Practice routine

For each of these seven kanji from Monday, write the kanji three times by hand, paying attention to stroke order. Say the meaning aloud as you write the third one.

三
四

六
七
Stroke order rule of thumb: Most kanji are written top to bottom, left to right. Horizontal strokes typically come before vertical. Boxes are drawn as left side first → top → bottom → right side closes the box.

Stroke counts to keep in mind:

一1 stroke · L→R
二2 strokes · top first
三3 strokes · top to bottom
四5 strokes
五4 strokes
六4 strokes
七2 strokes · cross then hook
If you don't remember stroke order for a specific kanji, write it however feels natural — perfect stroke order is a luxury for N5. The motor act of writing is the goal, not perfect calligraphy.

When you've finished writing, look at your paper. Are any of them surprising? Sometimes the act of writing reveals which kanji you weren't quite sure about. Note those — they're the ones to refresh on Friday's quiz day.

PART 02

New kanji — 八 九 十 百 千 万

~12 min

Six new kanji today, completing the numbers set. After today, you'll know all 13 of Week 1's kanji. The first three (八 九 十) finish the single digits; the last three (百 千 万) introduce the orders of magnitude — hundred, thousand, ten thousand. Note that 万 is unusual — Japanese counts in groups of 10,000, not 1,000 like English, so once you know it, all big numbers become accessible.

八
Number 8 of 104
eight
音ハチ
訓やっ(つ) / よう

Mnemonic

Two strokes splitting open downward — like the number 8 cut in half, or open scissor blades. Splitting = eight, the number that opens up many possibilities.

Stroke count

2 strokes (left stroke first, then right)

Example words

  • 八はち— eight
  • 八やっつ— eight (items)
  • 八はち月がつ— August
  • 八よう日か— 8th of month / eight days (irregular!)
Cultural note: 八 is considered a lucky number in Japan because the kanji shape widens at the bottom, suggesting prosperity that grows over time (suehirogari, 末広がり). The opposite of unlucky 四.
九
Number 9 of 104
nine
音キュウ / ク
訓ここの(つ)

Mnemonic

A flag bent over, almost ready to fall — nine is one short of ten, almost complete. Or imagine a stick figure throwing its arm forward.

Stroke count

2 strokes

Example words

  • 九きゅう— nine
  • 九ここのつ— nine (items)
  • 九く時じ— 9 o'clock (note: く, not きゅう!)
  • 九く月がつ— September (く!)
Reading split: Like 七, 九 has two on'yomi readings — キュウ for general counting, and ク specifically for time (九時) and months (九月). The reading ク overlaps with 苦 (suffering, く), so 九 sometimes carries a faint unlucky association — but far less than 四.
十
Number 10 of 104
ten
音ジュウ / ジッ
訓とお / と

Mnemonic

A plus sign — ten is complete, the foundation of the decimal system. The horizontal stroke crosses the vertical, suggesting balance and completeness.

Stroke count

2 strokes (horizontal first, then vertical down through it)

Example words

  • 十じゅう— ten
  • 十とお— ten (items, no つ)
  • 十じゅう円えん— 10 yen
  • 十とお日か— 10th of month / ten days
Don't confuse with: 千 (せん, thousand) — they look very similar! 十 has a clean cross; 千 has a slanted top stroke that hooks over the cross. Watch this distinction carefully on test day.
百
Number 11 of 104
hundred
音ヒャク
訓—

Mnemonic

One (一) stacked on top of white (白) — a hundred white things, a vast white field. The 一 at the top represents "one" of these large units of 100.

Stroke count

6 strokes

Example words

  • 百ひゃく— hundred
  • 百ひゃく円えん— 100 yen
  • 三さん百びゃく— 300 (note: びゃく, not ひゃく!)
  • 八はっ百ぴゃく屋や— greengrocer (lit. "800 store")
From Tuesday's drill: remember the irregular hundreds — 300 さんびゃく · 600 ろっぴゃく · 800 はっぴゃく. The kanji 百 stays the same, but the reading shifts. This is one of your gap items — drill it.
千
Number 12 of 104
thousand
音セン
訓ち

Mnemonic

Like 十 (ten) but with a slanted hook on top — much more than ten, even bigger than a hundred. The slash at the top suggests crossing into a higher order of magnitude.

Stroke count

3 strokes (slanted top stroke first, then horizontal, then vertical)

Example words

  • 千せん— thousand
  • 千せん円えん— 1000 yen
  • 三さん千ぜん— 3,000 (note: ぜん!)
  • 八はっ千せん— 8,000 (doubled っ)
Like with 百, the thousands have a few irregular readings — 3,000 = さんぜん and 8,000 = はっせん. Less common on the test than the hundreds, but worth knowing.
万
Number 13 of 104
ten thousand
音マン / バン
訓—

Mnemonic

A flag waving over a vast quantity — ten thousand, "myriad." Originally pictographic of a swarm of bees or scorpion, suggesting innumerable.

Stroke count

3 strokes

Example words

  • 一いち万まん— 10,000 (always with いち!)
  • 一いち万まん円えん— 10,000 yen
  • 百ひゃく万まん— 1,000,000 (one million)
  • 万ばん年ねん筆ひつ— fountain pen
Critical rule: 万 is always preceded by a number, even for "ten thousand" — say いちまん, never just まん. Compare with 百 (which can stand alone as ひゃく) and 千 (which can stand alone as せん). 万 is special. Also note: Japanese counts in 10,000 groups, so 100,000 is じゅうまん ("ten ten-thousands") and 1,000,000 is ひゃくまん.

Drill 4 · The kanji you might confuse

Look at each pair carefully. What's the difference?

  1. 十 vs 千
  2. 八 vs 入 (you'll meet 入 in Week 7)
  3. 百 vs 白 (the bottom of 百 IS 白 — meaning "white")
十 (じゅう, ten) has a clean cross. 千 (せん, thousand) has a slanted hook on top. · 八 (eight) splits open at the bottom; 入 (enter) is similar but the right stroke goes UNDER the left at the top. · 百 = 一 + 白 (one + white) = hundred. The components themselves are meaningful.
